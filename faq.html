Questions, answered honestly.

What kinds of dogs do you work with?

Sensitive, shy, anxious, fearful, reactive, or misunderstood dogs of all sizes. Maybe other walkers have been scared of your dog, unable to connect, or you’ve had bad experiences before. If so, I’m your gal.

What does “force-free” and “nervous-system-attuned” actually mean?

Force-free means I never use pain, fear, or intimidation: no prong or shock collars, no leash corrections, no dragging a scared dog past something terrifying. Nervous-system-attuned means I read your dog’s stress signals in real time and adjust the walk as we go: the route, the pace, how much space we leave around whatever worries them. The goal is to keep them in the zone where they feel safe enough to take things in and enjoy the walk.

What’s the meet-and-greet like?

Low-key. We meet somewhere calm and neutral, on your dog's terms, and see how everyone feels. Some dogs take the whole 30 minutes just deciding whether to sniff me, and that is completely fine. No long-term commitment required.

What if my dog has never warmed up to a new walker?

Totally okay. We go as slowly as they need. Trust builds at their pace, and we let it take the time it takes. Some dogs settle in about ten days; others need three weeks or more. It varies by the dog, and patience is what makes it work.

Why don’t you offer one-off walks?

Because they don’t work for sensitive dogs. A fearful dog meeting a new person once gains nothing and may lose ground. Weekly rhythm with the same trusted person is how these dogs actually make progress. It’s the difference between an interruption in their day and a relationship in their life.

My dog is reactive to other dogs. Can you still walk them?

Very likely yes. Dog-reactive dogs are a big part of my practice. All walks are solo (never group) and I choose routes and times that keep your dog under threshold, building their confidence at a distance they can handle. Reactive work depends on commitment, though: real progress needs a steady weekly rhythm, so it's best for pet parents ready to invest the time. If your dog ever needs more than walks can offer, I'll tell you and point you toward a trusted behavior specialist.

What if my dog has a bite history?

Welcome here, without judgment. A bite in the past usually says more about a moment a dog was overwhelmed than about who they are. Tell me about it honestly so I can keep everyone safe and set your dog up to succeed.

Which neighborhoods do you cover?

Maple Leaf, Roosevelt, Wedgwood, Ravenna, Fremont, and Wallingford in North Seattle. On the edge of one of those? Just ask and I’ll tell you honestly whether I can make the schedule work.

What’s your experience and background?

More than ten years with fearful, reactive, and anxious dogs, from reactive-dog training programs in the Midwest to a practice built entirely around sensitive dogs here in Seattle. I work force-free and I’m always glad to share my full background, references, and how I’d approach your dog directly with you before you book.

What happens in bad weather?

Seattle drizzle? We still head out, always within your dog’s tolerance. In genuinely unsafe conditions like ice, extreme heat, or smoke, we work indoors at your home instead: decompression, trust, and training skills matter more than mileage. If that isn’t possible, we reschedule. Your dog’s safety always comes first.

How do you get into my home?

Most clients use a lockbox or door code; I treat keys and codes with bank-level care.

What’s your cancellation policy?

Life happens. Give me 24 hours’ notice and there’s no charge; same-day cancellations are billed at half rate. If I ever have to cancel, that walk is free to reschedule and I’ll give you as much notice as humanly possible.

Still wondering if we’re a fit?

There’s no wrong question, and no judgment here. The easiest way to find out is a low-key, no-commitment meet-and-greet.
