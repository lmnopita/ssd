There’s more to your dog than ‘reactive.’

A lot of dogs get called reactive or difficult when the real story is different: they’ve had too little time, too little consistency, or a hard start with someone who didn’t understand them. A dog who barks at strangers, freezes at the door, or needs twenty minutes to trust someone new isn’t broken. They’re asking for patience that most walks don’t make room for.

These are exactly the dogs I love. With time, consistency, and a real partnership with the people who love them, a worried dog can learn to feel safe again.

How we’ll work together

Relationship first.

Same person, every walk.

Your dog gets to know me, Lily, and only me. That familiarity is how a sensitive dog learns to feel safe.

Nervous-system-attuned.

I read how your dog is feeling and adjust as we go: pace, route, and distance from whatever worries them, so we stay inside their window of tolerance.

A note and map.

After each walk, a note and a map: photos, what we practiced, how your dog settled, and our route.

No fear, no force.

No prong or shock collars, no leash corrections, no dragging a frightened dog past what scares them. Your dog’s comfort and consent come before distance or control.

How it works

  1. Say hello.

    Send me a note with your dog’s name and what’s made walks hard so far. No worries if it’s hard to put into words.

  2. Mellow Meet-and-Greet.

    I’ll meet you and your dog somewhere calm and neutral for thirty minutes, no agenda. Your dog sets the pace — even if that’s just deciding whether to sniff me.

  3. We build a rhythm.

    If it’s a good fit, we set a steady rhythm of at least two walks a week and build trust, one walk at a time.
