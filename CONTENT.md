**Empathy section — "If walks are hard, you're in the right place"**
Some dogs aren't "easy walks." They freeze at the door. They bark at strangers, lunge at skateboards, or take twenty minutes to trust a new person. Maybe other walkers have given up on your dog — or been afraid of them.

I specialize in exactly these dogs. Not despite the sensitivity, but because of it. Fearful and reactive dogs aren't broken; they're overwhelmed. With patience, consistency, and a walker who reads their signals in real time, they can feel safe enough to be curious again.

**What makes this different (3–4 cards)**
1. *Same person, every walk.* Your dog builds a relationship with me — Lily — not a rotating roster of contractors. Consistency is how sensitive dogs learn to trust.
2. *Nervous-system-attuned.* I read your dog's activation threshold live and adapt — pace, route, distance from triggers — keeping every walk inside their window of tolerance.
3. *A note after every walk.* Photos, behavioral observations, what we practiced, how they regulated, and a map of our route. You'll know your dog better, too.
4. *Force-free, always.* No prong, no shock, no leash pops, no flooding. Calm guidance and real connection — that's the whole method.

**How it works (3 steps)**
1. *Say hello.* Text or send the form. Tell me your dog's name and what's made walks hard before.
2. *Mellow Meet-and-Greet.* I come to you for 30 minutes. Your dog sets the pace — some spend the whole visit deciding whether to sniff me. That's fine.
3. *We build a rhythm.* If it's a good fit, we set up your dog's weekly walk schedule (2+ walks per week) and start building trust, walk by walk.

**Story**
I've been walking dogs for over ten years, and somewhere along the way the "difficult" dogs became my favorites. The shy ones. The barkers. The dogs who hide behind their humans, who've been dropped by other walkers, who need twenty minutes of sniffing before they can say hello.

I started Seattle Sensitive Dogs because these dogs deserve more than someone willing to tolerate them. They deserve someone who genuinely delights in them — and who has the knowledge to help them feel safe.

**Philosophy — "Calm guidance. Real connection."**
My approach is force-free and grounded in how dogs' nervous systems actually work. Every dog has a window of tolerance — the zone where they can take in the world, learn, and enjoy themselves. Push past it and you get lunging, freezing, shutdown. My job is to keep walks inside that window, and to gently widen it over time.

That means: I watch your dog's body language continuously. I adjust distance, pace, and route in real time. I never punish fear. And I treat every walk as one more deposit in the trust bank.

## FAQ (faq.html)

H1: Questions, answered honestly.
(Question-formatted H2s for AEO; concise quotable answers)

1. **What kinds of dogs do you work with?**
Sensitive, shy, anxious, fearful, reactive, or misunderstood dogs of all sizes. Maybe other walkers have been scared of your dog, unable to connect, or you've had bad experiences before. If so, I'm your gal.

2. **What does "force-free" and "nervous-system-attuned" actually mean?**
Force-free means I never use pain, fear, or intimidation — no prong or shock collars, no leash corrections, no dragging a scared dog past something terrifying. Nervous-system-attuned means I read your dog's stress signals in real time and adjust the walk — route, pace, distance from triggers — so they stay in the zone where they feel safe enough to learn and enjoy themselves.

3. **What's the meet-and-greet like?**
Low-key. I come to you, meet your dog on their terms, and we see how everyone feels. Some dogs take the whole 30 minutes just deciding whether to sniff me. That's completely fine. No commitment required.

4. **What if my dog has never warmed up to a new walker?**
Totally okay. We go as slowly as they need. Trust builds at their pace — not mine, not yours. Some of my favorite client relationships started with a dog hiding behind the couch.

5. **Why don't you offer one-off walks?**
Because they don't work for sensitive dogs. A fearful dog meeting a new person once gains nothing — and may lose ground. Weekly rhythm with the same trusted person is how these dogs actually make progress. It's the difference between an interruption in their day and a relationship in their life.

6. **My dog is reactive to other dogs. Can you still walk them?**
Very likely yes — dog-reactive dogs are a big part of my practice. All walks are solo (never group), and I choose routes and times that keep your dog under threshold, gradually building their confidence at a distance they can handle.

7. **Which neighborhoods do you cover?**
Maple Leaf, Roosevelt, Wedgwood, Ravenna, Fremont, and Wallingford in North Seattle. On the edge of one of those? Ask — I'll tell you honestly whether I can make the schedule work.

8. **Are you licensed, bonded, and insured?**
Not yet — and I'd rather tell you that plainly than bury it. I'm an owner-operated business working toward my certifications (force-free training and pet first aid) with licensing and insurance in progress. What I can offer today: 10+ years of experience, references on request, and total transparency about how I work. This answer will be updated the day each box is checked. ⚠️ DRAFT-REVIEW wording
9. **What happens in bad weather?** ⚠️ DRAFT-REVIEW policy
Seattle drizzle? We walk (many dogs love it). Genuinely unsafe conditions — ice, extreme heat, smoke — and I'll swap the walk for indoor enrichment and decompression at your home, or reschedule. Your dog's safety always wins.

10. **How do you get into my home?** ⚠️ DRAFT-REVIEW policy
Most clients use a lockbox or door code; I treat keys and codes with bank-level care and confirm every entry/exit in your walk note.
